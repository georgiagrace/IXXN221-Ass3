Week seven

This week we focused on composition and affordances, which was very helpful for designing assignment 2.1. I feel like understanding the difference between gutters, margins, padding etc has always been a brain twister and I never have completely understood which one you are meant to use, so this was helpful getting insight into the different and how you are meant to use them. It showed me how important grids and spacing are for web design and how it can change the whole visual aspect of a webpage – ensuring there is cohesiveness and format is super important in a successful design. The other composition elements that were helpful and interesting to learn about were visual hierarchy, scale, white space and balance. I have used most of these compositions in the past but it was helpful to have them explained further. The affordances were the most interesting to learn about this week though because once again, it is something you just do – but never know or understand why. Learning about the mental models was really interesting because it makes so much sense and as a designer it is good to understand why you are adding specific elements and that no matter how much you want to be different or niche in your design, if you were to truly put the customer experience first, you would ensure that you use these mental models and affordances so that you’re customer will have a good experience – no matter the site.

Week ten

This week was all about assignment 2.2 – my CMS website. I thought I would find this project easy – but boy was I wrong. I had experience with CMS platforms before therefore I thought using a new one (Webflow) would be a similar experience, however I found it extremely challenging in many ways. It was an eye opener for me to realise how different designing on Figma vs designing on a CMS platform was, and I am glad I now know. I started off my project feeling good, trying to mimic my Figma design the best I could and it all seemed straight forward until the ‘technical’ parts come into play. The filtering system for my service provider platform was the main compartment I needed to ensure was good, however it took me many tries and many different ways to make it the way I wanted. Webflow uses a lot of custom code and since I don’t know how to code, I was searching for a way to do it without code. I found a way to filter through different ‘locations’ and ‘services’ without code, however, this limited me to only being able to filter one at a time (for example, I couldn’t filter Auckland Cleaners, only Cleaners or any service providers in Auckland). I knew I couldn’t continue with this system because it is not user-friendly and if I was on the other end using this site – I wouldn’t. I kept looking for different ways and eventually, I purchased a premium Webflow account so I could add in custom code, I found a tutorial on how to use the checklist element on Webflow and therefore, I successfully created my minimal filtering system. I thought that would be the end of my stress – then I started with making my website responsive, this is when things got even more tricky. I had a vision of how I wanted my filtering system to look like on tablets and mobile from previous websites I have experienced and what is common, however, implementing this is a whole other situation. I really could not figure out how to do this on Webflow after watching even multiple videos and searching the web for help. There were many pages I designed on Figma that I did not have the time to complete on Webflow which did frustrate me at the end, I didn’t realise how long I would spend my time on the little things. Designing on Webflow was a very humbling experience, no matter how good you are at designing, there will always be platforms that trip you up. This project definitely made me grow as a designer and learn a lot of patience. Going forward, I want to practice and learn Webflow because I think it is an amazing CMS platform (if you know what you are doing).
